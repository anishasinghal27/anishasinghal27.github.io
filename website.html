Literature Review · September 30, 2025

Artificial Intelligence & Labor

A synthesis of the academic debate on how AI is reshaping the value and nature of human work.

Anisha Singhal

I conducted a literature review to better understand the academic perspectives of the AI as a complement or substitute for labor debate. My research spanned three main buckets: complement, substitute, and post-work. I compiled a number of theories on the future of labor, but the answer to which one is correct is not in the arguments but in the data. The insights from Anthropic's Economic Index are the next step to answer the question of how the theory applies to the real-world numbers.

Below is a sample of the theories I analyzed from various academics and industry experts. I have also included some of my commentary on them.

I. AI as a Complement to Labor

David Autor, Applying AI to Rebuild Middle-Class Jobs

Autor argues that AI, used well, could rebuild the middle-skill heart of the labor market hollowed out by automation and globalization. By making information abundant, AI could let a broader set of trained workers take on high-stakes decisions currently reserved for elite experts — doctors, lawyers, engineers, professors. The real danger, in his framing, is not a jobless future but the devaluation of expertise: to paraphrase The Incredibles, "if everyone is expert, no one is expert," labor then becomes disposable, and wealth accrues to the owners of AI. He also points out that the industrialized world is currently long on jobs and short on workers.

Most elite expert jobs have professional boards and licenses that create barriers to entry, so how will those interest groups and regulations impact the adoption of AI? How does the rate of decrease in jobs from AI compare to birthrate declines, and could they converge to an equilibrium state?

Lawrence Lundy-Bryan, Dirty Work: The Return of Blue Collar

Lundy-Bryan contends that our status hierarchy is inverted: the more necessary the work, the less respect it receives. White-collar jobs have bloated and become increasingly abstractive. Since AI can take over mental labor more easily than physical labor, it may invert this hierarchy. AI reduces meaningless abstraction through automation and agents and can push people toward more directly impactful jobs.

I have a theory that the more professionally you dress, the less societally important your job is. Those with important work need to get things done and need to be comfortable doing it. (This theory may be a product of conformation bias because I don't want to buy expensive work clothes.)

James Bessen, Automation and Jobs: When Technology Boosts Employment

Bessen's contribution is to make the employment effect of automation conditional on demand elasticity across an industry's life cycle. Early on, productivity gains cut prices enough to expand demand (elasticity above one) and total employment rises despite less labor per unit. As markets saturate and demand turns inelastic, the industry sheds workers. Faster technical change adds jobs where needs are unmet and destroys them where markets are mature.

Growth stage companies will be able to accelerate labor faster due to AI and easy access to capital through venture capital. Mature companies will be able to reduce bureaucratic bloat.

Richard Baldwin, Globotics and Development

Writing in 2020, Baldwin suggested that digital technology + globalization ("globotics") is reshaping development: robotics cuts manufacturing jobs (making some manufacturing jobless), while digital trade and telepresence make many services freely tradable — so development may shift from a manufacturing-led path (China) to a service-led path (India/Philippines).

I read the impact of AI as an inversion of that trend: it has made many services easier to automate than manufacturing.

Arvind Narayanan & Sayash Kapoor, AI as Normal Technology

This essay treats AI as a controllable tool rather than a human-like intelligence, and argues its societal impact is curbed less by raw capability than by diffusion. Method breakthroughs do not translate directly into economic effect because that requires application development and adoption, both slowed by safety concerns and regulation. The policy implication is to invest in complements (AI literacy, open data) that let diffusion catch up to innovation.

I'm working at a bank this summer and have been shocked by how bad the data-controlled AI models are compared to the models I have gotten used to using at school. Many fields are handicapped in their inability to complete and outsource tasks because of firewalling and lack of integration of the technology that is available.

II. AI as a Substitute for Labor

Erik Brynjolfsson et al., Canaries in the Coal Mine?

This paper finds that early-career workers (ages 22–25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have seen roughly a 13% relative decline in employment, while older workers in the same occupations and workers in less-exposed fields have held steady or grown. The mechanism is the gap between codified knowledge (which juniors bring and AI can automate) and tacit knowledge (which experience builds and AI cannot easily replace). The authors separate automating from augmenting tasks using Anthropic's Economic Index, and locate the employment effect mostly in the automating cases.

Noah Smith wrote a critique of this paper: "If AI is automating software engineering jobs, it makes sense that companies would not hire more fresh grads, but why would they be hiring more older software engineers? It is also odd that the wages for high risk jobs have not slowed and that the impact of AI is seen only in employment and not through wages." He also points out that the debate around is inherently skewed: "If AI takes a lot of people's jobs, the debate will end because one side will have clearly won. But if AI doesn't take a lot of people's jobs, then the debate will never be resolved, because there will be a bunch of people who will still go around saying that it's about to take everyone's job."

David Marguerit, Augmenting or Automating Labor?

This paper uses Stack Overflow activity to observe in near-real-time what AI tools developers are building, alongside changes in new job titles, employment, and wages. Marguerit finds that augmentation creates new work, slightly raises employment, and modestly lifts high-skill wages, whereas automation generates no new work or employment and pushes wages down.

Stack Overflow comments and activity is a creative method to codify automation vs. augmentation.

Erik Brynjolfsson & Andrew McAfee, The Second Machine Age

Where the first machine age made machines a complement to physical labor, the second makes them a substitute for cognitive labor. This leads to "bounty" (new abundance from accelerating innovation), alongside "spread" (a widening gap as substitution and talent-biased shifts skew income upward). The authors call for education that prizes creativity and adaptability over rote memorization, organizations redesigned around machine collaboration, better well-being metrics than GDP, and possibly a negative income tax or UBI.

This is the idea that AI will make few owners of production very wealthy while the rest of the workforce suffers.

Martin Ford, Rise of the Robots

Ford dates the rupture between productivity and wages to the 1970s — by 2013 the typical nonsupervisory worker earned about 13% less than in 1973 even as productivity rose 107%. Automation, he argues, is moving from "routine" to merely "predictable" tasks, threatening the feedback loop between productivity, wages, and consumer spending on which mass demand depends.

These types of economic analyses should be looked at with caution because they are highly sensitive to the metrics used.

III. Post-Work Perspectives

Aaron Benanav, Automation and the Future of Work

Benanav reframes the problem: today's weak labor demand stems not from a technological leap but from economic stagnation and global overcapacity that depress profits and output, leaving firms to survive by cost-cutting, offshoring, and automating. With services low-productivity and largely non-tradable, no new sector has replaced manufacturing as a growth engine. His answer is a move toward a post-scarcity settlement in which needs are guaranteed independently of wage labor.

The argument here is that competition is increasing, driving cost-cutting of all forms.

Jeremy Rifkin, The End of Work

Rifkin sees a steady, technology-driven decline in jobs across agriculture, manufacturing, and services, polarizing society between a tech-savvy elite and a displaced majority. He proposes three responses: a shorter (30-hour) workweek to spread available work, a guaranteed income paired with a work requirement, and a tax system that rewards time spent in the "third sector" of volunteering and non-profit work.

Time and time again the shortened work week has been suggested as the future of work. But why would you hire 10 3-day workers if you could hire 6 5-day workers instead? No business is incentivized to implement a 3 day work week.

Nick Srnicek & Alex Williams, Inventing the Future

This book embraces the post-work prospect outright: accelerate automation, shorten the workweek, implement a universal basic income, and dismantle the cultural primacy of the "work ethic." This idealistic, post-capitalist society would be organized around human flourishing rather than labor.

Sounds utopian, but it's easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism.

David Graeber, On the Phenomenon of Bullshit Jobs

Graeber observes that automation's gains never produced Keynes's promised 15-hour week. Instead, administrative and managerial bloat expanded to fill the time, generating jobs even their holders cannot justify, which he groups into flunkies, goons, duct-tapers, box-tickers, and taskmasters. Capitalism, supposedly an efficiency engine, accumulates meaningless work. The cruelty lies in the fact that genuinely useful jobs tend to pay least while the pointless ones pay best. It's hard to do meaningful work and get paid well for it.

AI can be a tool to get rid of some of these "bullshit" jobs, but it may also expand them. Managers may still demand the stamp of approval from a consulting firm. Middle management may stay in place for "quality control."

Conclusion

Read together, the literature is less a fight between optimists and doomsayers than a disagreement about which category a given technology falls into – augmentation or automation – and how fast diffusion will move it from one to the other. That distinction is the pivotal focus of empirical work of Anthropic's Economic Index, and it is the same question I take up in my Substack essays: how economic models break and reform as technology shifts from augmenting labor toward potentially replacing it, and how we should choose work when the underlying skills keep depreciating.

The data to settle this debate is being developed and harvested at Anthropic. Massenkoff and McCrory (2026) test the substitute-side hypothesis Brynjolfsson presents more directly and find it more muted than the "Canaries" results suggest: no systematic rise in unemployment among the most exposed workers since 2022, though hiring of 22-to-25-year-olds into exposed occupations has slowed by roughly 14%. Their observed exposure measure also captures that AI today reaches just a fraction of its theoretical capability, so the augmentation-heavy stage may be the transitional stage I describe in my essay on economic models.

The Economic Models Will Break (Again)

Economic models are designed for an era and become outdated. Changes across models can help predict where we are headed in the era of AI.

Anisha Singhal

A critical challenge with economic theory is that economic models become outdated. As society undergoes big cultural shifts and technological revolutions, current economic models become unreliable tools to predict movements in the next era of AI. However, looking at the changes across models over time can give us a sense of the direction in which we may be headed.

The Evolution of Economic Models

Ricardo Model, 1817

In his 1817 book "On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation" David Ricardo introduced the concept of comparative advantage (the idea that people, firms, or countries should produce not what they are best at producing but what they have the lowest opportunity cost of producing) and made three key assumptions:

  1. Labor is the only factor of production.
  2. There is a constant marginal product of labor (meaning each additional unit of labor can produce just as much as the last one).
  3. Labor is homogenous and all laborers earn wage (w).

This model was an economic breakthrough at the time. It may seem like an oversimplification of the world, but at the time it was not that much of a simplification. In the 17th century, labor drove production and modern forms of capital did not exist. Simply put: the more labor a firm could hire, the more clothes they could sell. A firm's output was primarily bounded by labor.

Ricardo-Viner Specific Factors Model, 1937

Long after the industrial revolution, Jacob Viner updated the Ricardian model with an important new factor: capital. Capital is a broad-ranging term that includes physical machines, technology, land, and financial assets. Viner realized that land and factories are specific factors that — unlike workers who migrated from rural to urban areas during the Industrial Revolution — are not mobile. A clothing factory cannot be used to make food, and corn fields cannot be used to make clothes. In Viner's addendum, there were 2 goods (let's use food and clothing) and 4 factors of production:

  1. Laborers in the food sector (mobile).
  2. Laborers in the clothing sector (mobile).
  3. Capital in the clothing sector (factories which are immobile).
  4. Capital in the food sector (land which is immobile).

We can see that as technology advanced, there were more factors to consider that fundamentally changed the way economists modeled the economy. A model is not necessarily changed because the old one was wrong, but because a new era constitutes a new kind of economy.

Stolper-Samuelson Mobile Factors Model, 1941

This model was developed during the same period as the Specific Factors model to represent the same economy in the long-run. However, it became more relevant as the nature of the goods being produced evolved. In this model, capital is mobile and labor is mobile but specialized. It assumed the following:

  1. There are 2 factors of production (capital and labor) and 2 goods (let's now use clothes and tech).
  2. The goods differ in the intensity with which they use the two factors.
    1. Tech uses more white-collar workers and little physical capital.
    2. Clothing uses more blue-collar workers and a lot of physical factories.
  3. In the long run, capital invested in the clothing industry can be moved over to the tech industry and vice-versa.

A key distinction here is that although the capital is mobile, labor is less mobile. Thus, there is a segregation between white-collar and blue-collar jobs. Both tech and clothing industries use white-collar and blue-collar workers, but tech uses more of the former and clothing uses more of the latter.

As education has become an increasingly important determinant of job type, it is highly unlikely that labor will move from white-collar to blue-collar and vice versa. Between the Specific Factors and Mobile Factors models, there has been a subversion in what inputs are flexible in an economy.

What is Next?

The models can be summarized as follows:

  1. Ricardo: Labor but no capital.
  2. Ricardo-Viner: Capital fixed, labor moves.
  3. Stolper-Samuelson: Labor fixed, capital moves.

Each economic era comes with a new, altered economic model. As such, the economic models work to explain the world within an era, but cannot predict how things will move in the new era of Artificial Intelligence.

Fear around job loss has haunted each new era. During the Industrial Revolution, there was fear that machines would replace workers, but in reality, the role of workers merely adapted. Similarly, as globalization took place, there was fear that manufacturing jobs would be outsourced to developing countries. This did happen, but the nature of the work in developed countries just shifted from manufacturing to services. Yesterday's textile mills were shut down and in their stead arose tech startups, healthcare services, and financial services.

Given these historical changes across economic models, there is a trend in the interactions of technology and labor. As we moved from the Ricardo model to the Ricardo-Viner model, technology became an essential element of production. As we moved from Ricardo-Viner to Stolper-Samuelson, labor became segregated into different pools to operate different forms of capital.

Today's model assumes that technology augments labor, boosts efficiency, and increases wages of the worker because they are able to produce more. The productivity of a country is still tied to its labor. Capital augments labor and makes it more efficient, but labor is still at the forefront of guiding production.

The present model does not, however, consider the case in which technology does not just augment one process, but replaces the white-collar, services worker. In the past, there was always some other sector to develop in place of the old one. But what comes after services? It remains unclear what sectors labor could move to in the future.

We already had an economic revolution in which labor became tied to a certain type of capital (blue-collar to clothes and white-collar to tech). If AI is able to do the same jobs that we do, then labor will either become more tethered to machines or it will be rendered obsolete.

In a world where the tech is better than the labor, I see two potential models emerging.

  1. We move from technology augmenting labor to labor augmenting technology. In other words, people work for machines.
  1. We move to the opposite of Ricardo's model (which only has labor as a factor) to a world in which labor is rendered obsolete and capital is the only factor.

In the first model, a country's comparative advantage will come from how well its labor uses technology in a specific sector. Highly skilled labor to prompt AI will be the driver of economic growth. However, the very nature of AI is that it can learn. It is not a tool to augment one process, but to replace it in all dimensions. So the first model may just be a stepping stone to the second one.

The economic models have and will continue to change. How much they will change and in what ways is unclear. The options look grim because this time, the progression into the new era seems likely to come at a cost to everyone except those at the top. They are the ones with the resources to create and deploy the capital over employing workers.

Working for a Product Rather than a Role

Instead of looking for jobs by industry, what if we looked for jobs at the companies with products we truly love and want to see succeed?

Anisha Singhal

The job search process for fresh grads has become increasingly predictable. We draw a line from a major → a field (medicine, law, business, tech). We define which roles we are after and try to land that role at the most prestigious company for that role. We research the ins and outs of a company before interviewing and point to a couple attributes that make that company special for the "Why Us?" question. But the company is typically a secondary consideration to the role.

A typical job searcher filters their priorities in the following order:

  1. Role
  2. Pay
  3. Company prestige
  4. Progression opportunities
  5. Company product

The order of those items varies by person and career stage, but the product is not typically the primary consideration. It was not always this way. Artisans were primarily concerned with their product and their craft. Karl Marx's theory of alienation of labor suggests that capitalist systems break the connection between workers and their output, which deprives us of meaning from our work.

In the modern era, it may be worth reviving product importance for two main reasons:

  1. Most roles are becoming outdated, so what is the value in your skillset anyways?
  2. You will be less inclined to care for and find fulfillment in the work you are doing if you don't care for the product you are working for.

If the specific skills you build are increasingly perishable, the only durable professional asset is caring enough to keep adapting. And that kind of sustained motivation is hard to manufacture. It tends to come from genuinely believing in what you're working for.

The skillset devaluation

In "The Career Ladder is Dead, the Career Garden is Here," Erin McGoff discusses the depreciating value of a specific skillset and role:

At Davos 2024, Accenture CEO Julie Sweet cited research showing the half-life of a professional skill used to be around 30 years. Now? It's closer to 5. The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report found that 39% of key skills in today's job market will change by 2030.

A few weeks ago, I was at the World Economic Forum in Davos speaking with LinkedIn CEO Ryan Roslansky — who, in my opinion, has one of the most rational takes on the future of work. He told me something that stuck: "Any job is a set of skills. AI doesn't replace people or jobs — it replaces skills."

Skills used to be a moat and a proxy for individual value in the corporate environment. Expertise and drive used to be good enough markers of a good employee.

My uncle likes to say that you can either optimize for expertise or money in your career. Ideally, expertise and money go hand-in-hand, but that may not always be the case. Sometimes pursuing expertise may mean sacrificing office politics, which would help you move up. Optimizing for money may mean giving up more interesting and meaningful work to focus on work that will boost your career more.

This presents another dilemma: optimizing for expertise may lead to more fulfillment in work, but if hard skills are becoming outdated, what can foster a more meaningful career? The substitute will be passion and adaptability.

Anthropic Economic Research · 2026

There's now data behind this. In a study of roughly 400,000 Claude Code sessions, Anthropic's Economic Research team found that what predicts success isn't coding proficiency but domain expertise: understanding the problem you're solving. This expertise is task-specific and durable where specific tool-skills are not. Coding agents don't substitute for that understanding, but reward it. When the skills expire, what's left is how well you understand the higher level purpose of the work itself.

The fulfillment fallacy

We have been conditioned to view work as a day job. We are told work-life balance is spending just 9am-5pm in an office and having 5pm-9pm to yourself. There is also an implicit tradeoff where less work-life balance may be compensated for with more pay (e.g. law, finance, consulting).

This is the wrong way to look at work. Work-life balance does not need to be a hard separation of work and life with designated time for each. Ideally, your work is something you want to be doing. A more meaningful breakdown of time and work would be getting back from work around 6:30pm, having dinner with your family (maybe discussing some of the things you are working on), then feeling inspired to log back on at 9pm to prepare new ideas to share the next day.

Maybe finding a corporate job that fills you with that much passion is a long shot. But finding a role working for a product, service, or idea you care deeply for should be the goal. It means looking beyond just what your boss asks of you and not just doing your job, but caring for the company holistically. Admittedly, not all corporate environments foster and appreciate that kind of passion. Beyond passion, culture is also a key feature of a fulfilling job.

This is not just a kumbaya search for meaning in a cold, capitalist world. A study by Oxford Professor Jan-Emmanuel De Neve found that Indeed's Work Wellbeing Score (which measures four key indicators of work wellbeing: happiness, purpose, satisfaction, and stress) has a strong positive correlation with stock performance. So much so that an ETF designed to track the firms with the highest wellbeing scores has consistently outperformed the S&P 500.

We increase shareholder value most when we care. We care when the product matters to us and we enjoy working with the people around us. As the job market evolves, choosing a company based on a product may not only be a pathway to professional fulfillment but also to job security.
